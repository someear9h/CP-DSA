# The 1000-Rated Problem Solver's Guide

## What Changes at 1000

At 900, almost every problem has one observation and the rest is implementation. At 1000, the observation is slightly less obvious, the implementation has one more moving part, or two simple ideas need to be combined. The gap is not massive but it is real.

The 80/20 here is: about 80% of 1000-rated problems are still one key observation, but that observation requires you to think one step deeper than the surface. The remaining 20% require two observations chained together. If you have internalized the 900 guide, you are already 60% of the way here. What this guide adds is the next layer.

---

## How 1000 Problems Differ From 900

At 900, small case computation almost always hands you the answer directly. At 1000, small cases show you the pattern but you often need one more reasoning step to prove why the pattern holds and generalize it.

At 900, the invariant is usually obvious once you think to look for it. At 1000, the invariant exists but is sometimes hidden one level deeper, like the parity of a sum rather than the sum itself, or the gcd of differences rather than the gcd of values.

At 900, greedy choices are straightforward. At 1000, greedy choices sometimes require an exchange argument you actually have to think through, not just feel.

At 900, constraints directly suggest the algorithm. At 1000, constraints still suggest the algorithm but you sometimes need to reframe the problem before the constraint becomes useful.

---

## The Expanded Thinking Process

The same checklist from 900 applies but with these additions inserted at each step.

**After reading the problem, ask: what is the real problem?**

1000-rated problems often describe something complex that reduces to something simple. A problem about moving items between boxes might really be about whether two numbers have the same parity. A problem about strings might really be about counting a specific character. Train yourself to ask "is there a simpler restatement of what this problem is actually asking?" before you do anything else.

**After computing small cases, ask: why does this pattern hold?**

At 900 you spot the pattern and code it. At 1000 you need to also ask why, because the why tells you how to handle edge cases and how to generalize. If the answer is always n-1, ask why one is always subtracted. If the answer alternates, ask what causes the alternation. The why is often the proof, and the proof is often the code.

**After identifying a candidate observation, stress test it mentally.**

Pick a case that feels like it might break your observation. Asymmetric input, all same values, n equals 1, n is very large, all values are maximum. If your observation survives these, code it. If it breaks, the broken case usually tells you what the real observation is.

## Topic-by-Topic Breakdown

### Math and Formulas

At 1000 the math problems require one more algebraic step than at 900. You still look for divisibility, parity, and floor patterns, but now you sometimes need to manipulate an expression before the pattern becomes visible.

The key habits:

Express the answer as a formula involving the input variables, even symbolically. Write f(n) = something, even if you do not know what something is yet. Then substitute your small case answers and solve for the formula. This is reverse engineering the math.

Look for telescoping. If a problem involves a sum or difference across a range, consecutive terms often cancel. Write out a few terms explicitly and check if adjacent ones collapse.

Ceiling and floor interplay. At 1000, problems often require you to know that ceil(a/b) = floor((a+b-1)/b). This lets you compute ceilings using only integer arithmetic, which matters in implementation.

The observation that covers many 1000-level math problems: if you are asked for the minimum or maximum of some expression over all valid inputs, the extremes are almost always achieved at boundary values, all same values, or alternating values. Check those three configurations first.

Modular arithmetic gets one step harder. At 900 you check divisibility. At 1000 you sometimes need to use the fact that if a is congruent to b mod m, then a*k is congruent to b*k mod m*k, or that you can add and subtract modularly but division requires modular inverse. Know when you can and cannot divide under a modulo.

### Number Theory

At 1000, number theory problems typically involve one of these deeper observations:

The number of divisors of n is O(n^(1/3)) on average and at most around 1500 for n up to 10^9. This means iterating over all divisors of a number by trial division up to sqrt(n) is always fast enough at this level.

If a problem asks you to find all numbers up to n with some divisibility property, think about iterating over multiples rather than iterating over numbers and checking. For each d from 1 to n, mark all multiples of d. This is the sieve mindset and it applies beyond just primes.

Euler's totient function counts numbers from 1 to n coprime to n. At 1000 you rarely need to compute it directly but you need to know that the sum of phi(d) for all divisors d of n equals n. This identity solves a surprising number of counting problems.

Prime factorization at 1000: if n is up to 10^6, precompute smallest prime factor with a sieve and factorize any number in O(log n). If n is up to 10^9, factorize by trial division up to sqrt(n) directly. Know which regime you are in.

The deeper GCD observation at 1000: gcd of an array does not change if you replace every element with the difference between consecutive elements. Formally gcd(a1, a2, a3, ...) = gcd(a1, a2-a1, a3-a2, ...). This is how you solve problems that ask about arithmetic progressions and common differences.

### Bit Manipulation

At 1000, bit manipulation problems require you to think about bits independently across positions. The key mental model is: treat each bit position as a completely separate problem and combine results.

The observations that cover most 1000-level bit problems:

AND of a subarray is non-increasing as the subarray grows. Once a bit turns off it never comes back. This means for any fixed right endpoint, as you extend the left endpoint leftward, the AND value only stays the same or decreases, and it can only decrease at most 30 times (once per bit). This is the key insight behind efficient AND subarray problems.

Same logic applies in reverse for OR: OR of a subarray is non-decreasing as it grows.

XOR problems at 1000 often exploit linearity. XOR is its own inverse, so a XOR a = 0 always. If you XOR all elements from 1 to n and separately XOR all elements of an array that should contain 1 to n, the result is the missing or extra element. Generalize this: if two sets differ by one element, XOR of both sets gives that element.

Maximizing XOR of two elements from an array: the answer involves finding the highest bit where two elements differ. Build a binary trie of all elements and greedily walk it choosing the opposite bit at each level. At 1000 you may not need the trie but you need the greedy bit-by-bit reasoning.

Bit masking for small n: if n is up to 20, consider representing subsets as integers and iterating over all 2^n subsets. At n=20 this is 10^6 which is fine.

### Greedy

At 1000, greedy problems require you to actually justify the greedy choice rather than just feel it. The justification is almost always one of two types.

Type one is the exchange argument. Assume you have an optimal solution that does not make the greedy choice at some step. Swap the greedy choice in and show the solution does not get worse. This proves greedy is safe. At 1000 you should be able to do this informally in your head in about 2 minutes.

Type two is the invariant argument. Show that making the greedy choice preserves some invariant that guarantees optimality. For example, always taking the smallest available element preserves the invariant that your current partial solution is lexicographically smallest.

The 1000-level greedy patterns that appear most often:

Interval problems. When you have intervals and need to select or cover them, sort by right endpoint. This is the canonical greedy for interval scheduling and it works because finishing earlier is always at least as good as finishing later.

Two-pointer greedy. When you need to pair elements from two sorted arrays to satisfy some constraint, use two pointers from opposite ends. This works when the problem has a monotone structure: if the current pair fails, moving one pointer inward cannot make things worse.

Prefix sum greedy. When you want to maximize or minimize a running total, the greedy choice is often to sort in a specific order so that the prefix sums behave well. Think about what happens to the partial sums as you reorder elements.

Contribution greedy. When each element contributes to the answer independently, compute each element's contribution and sort by it. Take the top k or threshold accordingly.

### Strings

String problems appear more at 1000 than at 900. The observations you need:

Frequency counting is the first tool. Almost every string problem at this level starts with counting character frequencies. Check if two strings have the same frequency vector before doing anything else.

Sorting characters and comparing is the second tool. If a problem asks whether you can transform one string into another, sort both and compare. Anagram-type reasoning.

At 1000, palindrome problems appear. The key observation: a string can be rearranged into a palindrome if and only if at most one character has an odd frequency. That is the entire observation for most palindrome possibility problems.

Prefix and suffix matching: if a problem involves finding where two strings agree or disagree, compute the longest common prefix directly. At 1000 you rarely need KMP or Z-algorithm but you need to think in terms of where strings first differ.

String problems that involve operations usually have an invariant. Ask what property of the string is preserved by the operation. Frequency of each character, relative order of characters, length, these are all candidates.

### Arrays and Sequences

At 1000, array problems go one level deeper than 900 in three ways.

Prefix sums become essential, not optional. Any problem involving subarray sums, range queries, or cumulative effects requires prefix sums. Define prefix[i] as the sum of the first i elements. Then sum of any subarray from l to r is prefix[r] minus prefix[l-1]. Compute this in your head automatically when you see subarray problems.

Difference arrays appear. If you need to apply range updates (add k to all elements from l to r) and then read final values, use a difference array. Increment diff[l] by k and decrement diff[r+1] by k. Then take prefix sum of diff to get the final array. This converts O(n) range updates to O(1) each.

Sorting to enable greedy. Many 1000-level array problems are unsolvable by greedy until you sort. After sorting, the structure becomes visible. Always ask: if I sort this array, does the problem become easier? Usually yes.

The median observation: if you need to minimize the sum of absolute differences between elements and a single value, that value is the median. This comes up in problems where you are moving elements to meet at a common point.

Counting inversions or out-of-order pairs: two elements are an inversion if the larger one comes first. The minimum number of adjacent swaps to sort an array equals the number of inversions. At 1000 you may not need to count them exactly but you need this concept.

### Two Pointers and Sliding Window

This technique appears frequently at 1000 and is underused by people still learning.

The signal: the problem asks about subarrays or substrings satisfying some condition, and the condition has a monotone property. That is, if a subarray of length k satisfies the condition, something predictable happens when you extend or shrink it.

The pattern: maintain a window with a left and right pointer. Extend right to include more elements. When the window violates the condition, advance left to shrink it. At each step, the window represents the best valid range ending at right.

At 1000, sliding window problems usually involve: maximum sum subarray of exactly length k, longest subarray with sum at most k, smallest subarray with sum at least k, or longest substring with at most k distinct characters.

The key is recognizing the monotone property. If adding an element to the window only makes the condition easier to violate, not harder, then two pointers works.

### Sorting and Binary Search on Answer

Binary search on the answer is a 1000-level technique that many people do not know exists.

The signal: the problem asks for a minimum or maximum value, and checking whether a given value is achievable is significantly easier than finding the optimal value directly.

The pattern: instead of finding the optimal answer directly, binary search over possible answers. For each candidate answer mid, ask "is mid achievable?" Write a separate function that answers this yes/no question. Binary search finds the boundary between yes and no.

This works when the achievability function is monotone: if mid is achievable, then all values on one side are also achievable. For minimization problems, if mid works, anything larger also works, so binary search for the smallest mid that works.

At 1000, the check function is usually O(n) or O(n log n), making the total complexity O(n log n) or O(n log^2 n), which is fine.

Examples of where this applies: minimum maximum load when distributing tasks, minimum time when workers work in parallel, smallest threshold that allows some condition to be satisfied.

## The Deeper Meta-Patterns at 1000

**Restate the problem before solving it.**

Many 1000-rated problems look hard in their original form and trivial in a restated form. Practice translating the problem into a different equivalent form. "Minimize the maximum" becomes "binary search on the answer and check feasibility." "Can you reach state B" becomes "find the invariant and check if A and B agree." "Count pairs with property P" becomes "for each element, count how many previous elements combine with it to satisfy P."

**Think about what you are really choosing.**

Many problems say you choose some subset or sequence. Ask: what is the actual degree of freedom? What choices genuinely affect the answer and what choices do not matter? Often you have less freedom than you think, which simplifies the problem. Or you have more freedom than you think, which lets you fix some choices arbitrarily.

**Decompose into independent subproblems.**

At 1000, some problems can be split by index (handle odd indices and even indices separately), by bit (handle each bit position independently), or by value (handle each possible value independently). If the problem has some separability structure, exploit it.

**The pigeonhole gets sharper.**

At 1000, pigeonhole arguments are more precise. If you have n+1 numbers each between 1 and n, two are equal. But also: if you have numbers summing to S and there are k of them, at least one is at least S/k. At 1000, this averaging argument appears in problems about distributing items, balancing loads, and guaranteeing certain element values.

**Work backwards.**

If the problem asks about a final state after operations, sometimes it is easier to think about what state you must have started from to reach the desired final state. Reverse the operations and ask: starting from the desired output, what inputs produce it?

## The Two-Observation Problems

About 20% of 1000-rated problems require two observations. The structure is always: observation one simplifies or transforms the problem, and observation two solves the simplified version.

The way to handle these is to solve the first layer and then treat what remains as a fresh problem. People fail these by trying to hold both observations in their head simultaneously before they have found either one.

Concretely: make the first observation, restate the problem in its simplified form, then apply your full checklist to the new problem from scratch.

## What to Practice and How

Solve 1000-rated problems with a hard 45-minute limit. No hints, no editorial within those 45 minutes.

After 45 minutes with no solution, do not read the editorial. Instead, read only the first one or two lines of the editorial, which usually contain the key observation without the full solution. Close the editorial. Try to solve it from that observation.

After solving (or after fully reading the editorial), write three things: the key observation in one sentence, what in the problem should have signaled that observation, and one other problem type where the same observation would apply.

After 30 problems done this way, you will find that 1200-rated problems start feeling like 1000 felt at the beginning. The skill transfers upward consistently because the thinking process is the same, just applied to slightly deeper observations.

The goal at 1000 is not to memorize more patterns. The goal is to make your thinking process fast and systematic enough that you find the observation within 15 minutes on problems you have never seen before. That is the actual skill. Everything else is a proxy for it.
